# Factions of the City

Inspired by the faction system of Waterdeep. The city runs on competing interests — no single group controls everything, and the Badger's End sits at the crossroads of all of them.

## The Badger's End — Neutral Ground

The Badger's End is not a faction but functions like one. Mr. H maintains its neutrality deliberately — all factions are welcome, none are favored. This makes it the most politically significant building in the city that isn't a seat of power.

Every faction knows this. Every faction respects it. Violating the tavern's neutrality would be a declaration of war against every other faction simultaneously.

## Power Struggles

### The Port (Merchant Guild vs. The Underworld)
The docks are the most contested territory in the city. The Merchant Guild controls legitimate trade; the Underworld controls what moves in the dark. Both want more of what the other has. The city government collects taxes from one and bribes from the other and pretends not to notice the difference.

### The Poor Quarter (The Temple vs. The City)
The cleric serves people the city ignores. This gives the Temple information and loyalty the city can't buy. The city leader wants the Temple's cooperation; the Temple wants the city to actually help its people. Neither fully gets what they want.

### Oversight of Magic (The Arcane Circle vs. The City)
The city leader wants visibility into what the Arcane Circle is doing. The Arcane Circle wants to be left alone. This is a cold tension — polite, occasionally cooperative, fundamentally opposed.

### The Hidden Hand (The Masked vs. Everyone)
The Hidden Council pulls strings across all factions. Some faction leaders know they exist. Most don't know who they are. The party will uncover this slowly — different DMs can reveal different threads.

### Wild Card — The Fae
Mr. H came from the Fae Wild. That connection doesn't end at the city gates. If something from the Fae takes interest in the city — or in him — every faction will have to respond to something none of them understand.

## Player Allegiances

Players can align with one or more factions over the course of the campaign. Allegiance is tracked in the world-state doc and has real consequences.

### Renown Scale

| Renown | Standing | What It Means |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| 0 | Unknown | The faction doesn't know you exist |
| 1–2 | Noticed | They're aware of you; cautious interest |
| 3–5 | Associate | Trusted enough for small favors and basic information |
| 6–8 | Member | Real access; faction resources available; expected loyalty |
| 9–11 | Proven | Significant trust; sent on important work; faction protects you |
| 12+ | Inner Circle | Access to leadership; faction secrets; major obligations |

### Gaining Renown
- Completing a mission for a faction: +1 to +3 depending on importance
- Solving a problem that benefits the faction without being asked: +1
- Publicly aligning with the faction in a meaningful situation: +1

### Losing Renown
- Working against a faction's interests: −1 to −3
- Betraying a faction secret: −4 or more
- Helping a rival faction at the first faction's expense: −2

### Allegiance Conflicts
Players can hold allegiances to multiple factions — but factions notice when their allies serve their rivals. At high renown with competing factions, a loyalty test becomes inevitable.
