# The Hidden Council — "The Masked"

> *The city has a leader. The city also has owners. These are not the same people.*

---

## Inspiration

Directly inspired by the Lords of Waterdeep — a secret ruling body whose identities are unknown to the public and to most of the factions they influence. Unlike Waterdeep's Lords, the Hidden Council does not necessarily have benevolent intentions, and their existence is not an open secret — most people in the city don't know they exist at all.

---

## What They Are

A small group — probably three to seven individuals — who have been quietly coordinating the city's direction for years, possibly decades. They do not rule openly. They fund, influence, threaten, and occasionally arrange. The city leader may be a member, a puppet, or genuinely unaware of them. That answer should emerge through play.

Their membership likely crosses faction lines — a senior merchant, someone in the Temple, possibly a figure from the Arcane Circle, possibly someone connected to the Underworld. They are united not by ideology but by shared interest in the city's stability and profitability — on their terms.

---

## What They Want

The city to remain exactly as it is: profitable, functional, and under their quiet management. They are not villains in the simple sense. They believe they are better stewards of the city than any democratic or official process would produce. They may even be right about some of this.

What they will not tolerate: anything that threatens the balance they've built — whether that's an external threat, an ambitious faction leader, or a party of adventurers pulling on threads they shouldn't.

---

## What They Know

Everything worth knowing. They have contacts in every faction, informants in the guard, donors in the Temple, and financial leverage over the Merchant Guild. Information flows toward them and doesn't flow back out without reason.

---

## How to Reveal Them — DM Guidance

The Hidden Council should be discovered gradually across multiple sessions and multiple DMs. No single DM reveals the whole picture. Each DM can add one thread:

**Early hints (sessions 1–5):**
- Decisions get reversed for no official reason
- A guard investigation stops suddenly
- The city leader seems to be responding to someone else's priorities

**Mid-game signals (sessions 6–12):**
- The party finds a message that references the Council obliquely
- An NPC they trust mentions "certain arrangements" that keep the city stable
- Someone warns the party off a line of investigation — politely, then firmly

**Late revelation (sessions 12+):**
- A member approaches the party directly — they've become too useful (or too dangerous) to ignore
- The party uncovers a meeting, a document, or a financial trail
- An internal Council conflict breaks into the open and the party is caught between sides

## The Council and the Fae

One thread worth planting early: did the Hidden Council have anything to do with Mr. H's arrival? Are they interested in a Satyr from the Fae Wild settling into the city's most politically neutral building? Do they know something about Mr. Hoots?

This doesn't need an answer yet. But it's a good question to leave in the air.

---

## Adventure Hooks

- The party is approached with a job offer from an anonymous client — the money is extraordinary and the instructions are very specific
- Someone who was investigating "certain arrangements" has gone quiet
- A Council member breaks ranks — they want out, and they want the party's help
- The party realizes that a string of events across several sessions was being orchestrated
- The Council decides the party has become a variable that needs to be managed

---

## DM Notes

The Hidden Council is a long game. No single DM should reveal too much. After each session, update the "Revealed?" column if the party made meaningful progress.

The Council works best as an explanation for why things that should work, don't — and why things that shouldn't happen, do. They are the city's immune system, and the party is something it hasn't quite classified yet.
