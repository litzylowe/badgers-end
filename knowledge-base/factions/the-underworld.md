> *We don't break the law. We operate in the space between laws.*

## What They Want

Profit, territory, and the freedom to operate without interference. The Underworld is not chaotic — it runs on rules, reputation, and the reliable delivery of services that the legitimate economy won't provide. They want the city stable enough to be profitable, and lawless enough not to ask too many questions.

They don't want war with the other factions. War is bad for business. They want arrangements.

---

## Structure *(Placeholder — define as the campaign develops)*

The Underworld's leadership is deliberately left open. Options:

- **Single Boss Model** — One figure controls everything. Efficient, stable, and very dangerous to cross.
- **Council Model** — Several gang or guild leaders in uneasy alliance, each controlling a territory (docks, shady quarter, the road trade, etc.). More volatile; internal power struggles create adventure hooks.
- **Shadow Broker Model** — Leadership is unknown even within the organization. Orders come through intermediaries. Inspired by Waterdeep's Xanathar.

*Recommended starting point: Council Model with a rumored figure above the council that nobody has confirmed.*

---

## What They Control

- The shady quarter — businesses, housing, protection arrangements
- Smuggling through the port (what moves after dark)
- A network of informants throughout the city, including in legitimate institutions
- Fencing of stolen goods
- Muscle for hire, off the books

---

## What They Can't Control

- The city guard's periodic crackdowns (inconvenient but survivable)
- The Merchant Guild's expanding dock presence
- The Temple — they've learned not to interfere with healers
- Their own members' ambitions

---

## The Underworld's Code

Not every criminal organization has rules, but functional ones do. Suggested baseline — adjust as the campaign establishes it:

- Don't touch the Temple. Bad for reputation, bad for access to healing.
- The Badger's End is neutral ground. Nobody works there.
- Debts are honored. Always.
- You don't sell out members to the guard. Ever.

Violations of the code are handled internally. Messily.

## Player Allegiance — The Shadow

**How to earn renown:** Complete jobs that can't be official, recover things the Shadow has lost, eliminate threats to their operations, prove you can keep your mouth shut.

**Benefits by renown:**

| Renown | Benefit |
|--------|---------|
| 1–2 | Access to the fence (sell questionable items); a safe house address |
| 3–5 | Black market goods available; information on city criminal activity |
| 6–8 | Muscle available for one job; the Shadow intervenes in one legal problem |
| 9–11 | Shadow contacts in every major institution; significant illegal resources |
| 12+ | An arrangement — the party operates under Shadow protection; significant obligations |

**Allegiance cost:** Working with the city guard against Shadow operations, exposing Shadow members, or interfering with Shadow territory damages standing severely. High Shadow renown makes high City renown increasingly difficult to maintain simultaneously.

**Important — Allegiance Conflict:** High renown with both The Shadow and The Shield is eventually unsustainable. A loyalty test will come.

## Adventure Hooks

- A Shadow job goes wrong and the party is holding evidence that could burn everyone involved
- Someone inside the Shadow is selling information to the Merchant Guild — the Shadow wants it stopped
- A new player is moving into the shady quarter and the existing structure is fracturing
- The party is asked to find someone who disappeared after crossing the Shadow — is it a rescue or a warning?
- A Shadow figure approaches with information the party desperately needs — the price is uncomfortable

---

## DM Notes

The Underworld is most compelling when it has a face. The first Shadow NPC the party meets and develops a relationship with should be logged in the world-state doc as a candidate for anchor status — this is the faction most likely to produce a beloved randomized NPC that graduates to anchor.

Play the Underworld as professional, not sadistic. They hurt people when it's necessary and profitable. Gratuitous cruelty is bad management.
